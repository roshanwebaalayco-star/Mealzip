GUIDELINE 1 — EAT VARIETY OF FOODS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

No single food provides all essential nutrients. A variety of foods ensures nutritional adequacy. Indians should consume foods from all food groups daily:
- Cereals and millets (staple): provide energy, B vitamins, fibre
- Pulses and legumes: protein, iron, zinc, folate
- Vegetables and fruits: vitamins, minerals, fibre, antioxidants
- Milk and dairy: calcium, protein, B12, vitamin D
- Fats and oils: fat-soluble vitamins, essential fatty acids
- Flesh foods (non-vegetarian): high-quality protein, haeme iron, B12, omega-3

Indian dietary pattern is predominantly cereal-pulse based and mostly vegetarian. Diversity within plant foods is crucial for vegetarians to ensure B12, iron, zinc, calcium adequacy.

GUIDELINE 2 — ENSURE PROVISION OF GOOD QUALITY PROTEINS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Protein requirement for Indians:
- Reference protein (egg protein) digestibility-corrected: 0.83g/kg/day
- Sedentary adult male: 54g/day
- Sedentary adult female: 46g/day
- Pregnant women: 78g/day (additional 24g over normal)
- Lactating women (0-6 months): 74g/day

Protein quality: Indian vegetarian diets are often deficient in essential amino acids. Combining cereals + pulses improves protein quality (complementation):
- Dal-roti, dal-chawal, idli-sambar are ideal complementary combinations
- Soybean is the only plant food with complete protein profile

For growing children, protein is critical for brain development and growth. Children aged 4-6 years need 20g/day; 7-9 years need 29g/day.

GUIDELINE 3 — TAKE PLENTY OF VEGETABLES AND FRUITS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Indians should consume at least 400g vegetables and fruits per day (WHO recommendation).
- Dark green leafy vegetables (DGLV): palak, methi, bathua, sarson, moringa — rich in iron, calcium, folate, vitamin A, vitamin C
- Yellow-orange vegetables: carrot, sweet potato, pumpkin — rich in beta-carotene (provitamin A)
- Citrus fruits: amla (highest vitamin C of any fruit — 600mg/100g), lemon, orange, guava
- Seasonal fruits: papaya, banana, mango, jamun (antidiabetic properties)

Amla (Indian gooseberry) is exceptional:
- Vitamin C: 600mg/100g (vs. orange: 53mg/100g)
- Enhances iron absorption from plant sources by 2-3 fold
- Antioxidant, anti-inflammatory properties

Dark green leafy vegetables should be consumed daily. Cooking destroys some vitamin C — consume raw or minimally cooked.

GUIDELINE 4 — CHOOSE WHOLE GRAINS AND MILLETS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Millets are climate-resilient, nutrient-dense traditional crops of India. ICMR-NIN 2024 strongly promotes millets as part of the Indian plate:

Nutritional composition of major millets (per 100g dry weight):
- Ragi (finger millet): Calcium 344mg, Iron 3.9mg, Protein 7.3g — highest calcium of all cereals
- Bajra (pearl millet): Iron 8.0mg, Protein 10.6g, Zinc 3.1mg — excellent for anaemia prevention
- Jowar (sorghum): Calcium 25mg, Iron 4.1mg, Protein 10.4g — good for diabetes management
- Rajgira (amaranth): Calcium 215mg, Iron 7.6mg, Protein 14.0g — complete protein
- Kuttu (buckwheat): GI 54, Protein 13.3g — suitable for diabetics and fasting

Whole grains vs refined grains:
- Whole wheat: contains bran, germ, endosperm — retains fibre, B vitamins, minerals
- White rice: polished, bran and germ removed — lower nutritional value
- Brown rice: GI 50 (vs white rice GI 64-72) — better for diabetes management

Recommendation: Replace 50% of refined cereal intake with whole grains and millets.

GUIDELINE 5 — REDUCE SALT INTAKE
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

WHO recommendation: < 5g salt per day (< 2g sodium)
Current Indian average: 8-10g salt per day (double the safe limit)
High salt increases risk of: hypertension, cardiovascular disease, stroke, kidney disease

Major salt sources in Indian diet:
- Table salt added in cooking (50% of daily intake)
- Pickles (achar): 15-30% salt by weight — major contributor
- Packaged foods: chips, namkeen, biscuits, instant noodles
- Papads: high sodium content
- Processed meats (salami, sausages)

Practical reduction strategies:
- Use lemon, tamarind, dry mango powder (amchur) for sour flavor — reduces salt need
- Use herbs: coriander, mint, curry leaves, ginger — flavour without sodium
- Avoid packaged namkeen, chips — use roasted chana, makhana as snacks
- Choose low-sodium versions of pickles or make at home

GUIDELINE 6 — USE FATS AND OILS IN MODERATION
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Daily recommended fat intake for adults: 25-30g/day (3-4 teaspoons)
Total fat should contribute 20-30% of total energy intake.

Indian cooking oils — composition and recommendations:
- Mustard oil (sarson ka tel): rich in omega-3 ALA, MUFA — preferred in North, East India
- Groundnut oil (moongphali tel): high MUFA, low in saturated fat — good for cooking
- Coconut oil: high in saturated fat — use in moderation, traditional in coastal regions
- Sesame oil (til ka tel): good ratio of MUFA:PUFA — traditional in South India
- Ghee: pure saturated fat — use sparingly (1-2 tsp/day maximum)

Oil rotation strategy (ICMR-NIN recommendation):
- Rotate between 2-3 oils from different fat categories
- Avoid using single oil exclusively for months
- Vanaspati (hydrogenated fat) and dalda: contain trans fats — avoid completely

Avoid deep frying. Prefer: sautéing (bhuna), steaming, boiling, roasting.

GUIDELINE 7 — RESTRICT SUGAR CONSUMPTION
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

WHO: Free sugars should be < 10% of total energy intake
For a 2000 kcal diet: < 50g sugar per day (< 12 teaspoons)
Strict target: < 5% (< 25g/day) for additional health benefits

Hidden sugar in Indian foods:
- Soft drinks: 8-10 teaspoons per 300ml can
- Packaged juices: 6-8 teaspoons per 250ml
- Biscuits: 2-4 teaspoons per 100g
- Mithai (Indian sweets): 20-40g sugar per piece
- Flavoured yogurt: 4-6 teaspoons per 100g

Healthier alternatives:
- Replace white sugar with jaggery (gud) — contains iron (11.4mg/100g), calcium
- Use dates as natural sweetener in desserts
- Reduce sugar in chai gradually (1 tsp → 0.5 tsp → none)
- Choose fresh fruit over fruit juice (fibre intact, lower sugar absorption rate)

GUIDELINE 8 — ENSURE SAFE AND CLEAN FOOD
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Food safety is critical for nutrition. Contaminated food leads to diarrhoeal diseases causing malnutrition, especially in children.

Key food safety practices:
- Wash hands before cooking and eating (minimum 20 seconds with soap)
- Wash fruits and vegetables thoroughly before consumption
- Store cooked food < 2 hours at room temperature; refrigerate at < 5°C
- Cook meat thoroughly (internal temperature > 74°C)
- Separate raw meat, poultry, seafood from ready-to-eat foods
- Boil water or use filtered water for drinking

Fermented foods (dahi, idli, dosa, kanji, dhokla) are safe, probiotic-rich, and increase bioavailability of iron, zinc.

GUIDELINE 9 — ADOPT HEALTHY LIFESTYLE
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Physical activity recommendations (WHO / ICMR-NIN):
- Adults: minimum 150 minutes moderate-intensity or 75 minutes vigorous activity per week
- Children 5-17 years: minimum 60 minutes moderate-to-vigorous activity daily
- Elderly: include balance exercises, muscle-strengthening activities

Sedentary behaviour is increasingly common in urban India. Even light activity (10-minute walk after meals) significantly reduces post-meal blood glucose by 20-30%.

India-specific lifestyle considerations:
- Early morning sun exposure: 15-30 minutes for vitamin D synthesis
- Yoga and pranayama: stress reduction — reduces cortisol which affects blood sugar and weight
- Adequate sleep: 7-9 hours for adults — sleep deprivation increases hunger hormones

Alcohol: linked to liver disease, hypertension, obesity, certain cancers. If consumed, keep to < 1 drink/day for women, < 2 drinks/day for men.

DIETARY CONSIDERATIONS FOR SPECIFIC HEALTH CONDITIONS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Diabetes Mellitus (Type 2):
- Glycaemic index (GI) focus: prefer low GI foods (< 55)
- Low GI Indian foods: oats, rajma, chana, lentils (masoor), bitter gourd, fenugreek
- High GI to limit: white rice, maida, potato, watermelon, white bread
- Fibre (25-30g/day) slows glucose absorption — key for blood sugar control
- Methi (fenugreek) has clinical evidence for reducing post-meal blood glucose
- Karela (bitter gourd): contains plant insulin (polypeptide-P) — clinical evidence for antidiabetic effect

Cardiovascular Disease:
- Reduce saturated fat (ghee, dalda) — replaces with unsaturated (mustard, groundnut oil)
- Increase omega-3: flaxseed (alsi), walnuts, fish (hilsa, sardine, mackerel)
- Increase fibre: oats, psyllium husk (isabgol) reduces LDL cholesterol
- DASH diet (Indian adaptation): high vegetables, fruits, whole grains, low sodium

Osteoporosis:
- Calcium: 1000-1200mg/day — from milk, dahi, ragi, sesame seeds, green leafy vegetables
- Vitamin D: enhances calcium absorption — 10-15 mcg/day from sunlight + fortified foods
- Magnesium: whole grains, nuts, seeds — supports bone density
- Avoid excessive salt: increases urinary calcium loss
